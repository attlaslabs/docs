Every company has valuable knowledge.

Processes. Documents. Training materials. Procedures. Contracts. Customer information. Internal policies.

The problem is that, most of the time, this knowledge exists but isn't easily accessible.

It's scattered across:

- PDFs
- Spreadsheets
- Internal systems
- Databases
- Shared documents
- Emails
- People

**Yes, people.**

Every company has employees who eventually become the primary source of information for everyone else.

When someone needs help, the solution is usually the same:

> "Ask John."

> "Sarah knows."

> "The HR team can help you."

At first, this seems normal.

But as the company grows, this model starts creating problems.

---

## The problem with information that exists but nobody can find

Most companies don't suffer from a lack of information.

Quite the opposite.

They have too much information.

The problem is that finding the right answer often requires time, context and frequently the help of another person.

Think about common workplace questions:

- What is the correct process for approving a contract?
- Where can I find the onboarding materials?
- What is the company's refund policy?
- How does the expense approval process work?
- Where is the latest version of this document?

The information exists.

But it isn't always easy to find.

And when that happens, teams develop a dangerous habit: **depending on each other to access knowledge.**

---

## The hidden cost of internal dependency

Every interruption seems small.

- A quick question
- A Slack message
- A short call

But when this happens dozens of times a day, the impact adds up.

People stop focusing on their own work to answer recurring questions.

- Managers become information hubs.
- Specialists spend part of their day explaining the same processes.
- New employees take weeks or even months to understand how everything works.

The result is:

- Lower productivity
- More interruptions
- More rework
- Slower onboarding
- Excessive dependence on specific people

Many companies believe this is simply part of growth.

**But it shouldn't be.**

---

## Every company has invisible experts

There is a common phenomenon in organizations of all sizes.

Some people simply know everything.

They understand:

- Processes
- Systems
- Customers
- Internal procedures
- Rules that were never documented

These people become indispensable.

And that sounds great.

**Until they go on vacation, move to another team, or leave the company.**

Suddenly, critical information disappears with them.

The problem isn't a lack of knowledge.

The problem is that knowledge is concentrated in people rather than being accessible to the organization.

---

## What happens when knowledge becomes accessible

Imagine a company where any employee can find answers without depending on someone else.

A company where processes, documents and information can be accessed through a simple conversation.

A company where knowledge isn't trapped inside complex systems or concentrated in the hands of a few employees.

This is exactly the transformation a knowledge hub is designed to create.

**The goal isn't to replace people.**

It's to allow them to spend less time searching for information and more time creating value.

## One chat or multiple chats?

One of the most common questions is whether a company should create a single chat or split knowledge across multiple chats.

The answer depends on the structure of the organization.

Smaller companies can usually centralize everything in a single company-wide chat.

Larger organizations often achieve better results by organizing knowledge by department.

For example:

- Corporate chat: General company information
- HR chat: Benefits, vacation policies, onboarding and internal policies
- Sales chat: Proposals, sales materials and commercial processes
- Operations chat: Workflows, procedures and checklists
- Support chat: Technical documentation and troubleshooting

This structure helps keep knowledge organized and makes it easier for employees to find what they need.
